How Long Does It Take to Sell a House in Stamford, CT?

Current days on market, the full list-to-close timeline, and what sellers can do to move faster.

Last updated · July 2026

In Stamford's current market, plan on roughly ~38 days on market to get under contract, then another ~44 days to close — about 80 days total from list date to keys handed over.

Short answer: ~38 days on market + ~44 days to close = ~80 days list-to-close for a financed sale. Well-priced homes in hot neighborhoods sell faster. Cash deals can close in 2–3 weeks once under contract.

What does the Stamford home selling timeline actually look like?

  1. Prep and listing (1–3 weeks before going live): Photos, video, staging, disclosures, pricing strategy. Rushing prep typically costs money — buyers notice.
  2. Active on market (~38 days average): Showings, open houses, and offer review. Well-priced homes can go under contract in the first weekend; overpriced homes sit.
  3. Under contract → closing (~44 days for financed buyers): Inspection, buyer's appraisal, mortgage underwriting, attorney title work, and scheduling closing.
  4. Closing day: Sign the deed, receive the wire, hand over the keys.

How can I sell faster in Stamford?

The most impactful things a seller can control:

  • Price it right from day one. The first 10 days are your highest-traffic window. Overpriced homes that later reduce often net less than homes priced correctly at launch.
  • Professional photos and video. Most buyers see your home online first — your listing photos are the first showing.
  • Declutter and do light staging. Buyers decide in minutes whether they can see themselves in a home.
  • Complete small repairs before listing. Inspectors find everything — get ahead of it to avoid renegotiation delays after you're under contract.
  • List Thursday or Friday. Homes that launch Thursday and Friday capture that weekend's full showing traffic.

What slows down a sale in Stamford?

Common culprits: overpricing (reduces showings and extends days on market), deferred maintenance that surfaces in the inspection and triggers price reductions or repairs, title issues that a local attorney needs time to clear, and buyer financing that runs into underwriting complications. A good listing agent front-loads the work that prevents these delays.
