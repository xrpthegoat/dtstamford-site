How Much Is Rent in Stamford, CT? (2026)

Median asking rent, the cheapest tier, and exactly how many apartments sit under $1,800, $2,200, and $3,000 right now — from the live Stamford-area MLS, not a stale annual average.

Updated live · refreshed every ~3 hours

The median asking rent in Stamford, CT is about $3,850/mo, but that headline number is skewed upward by luxury waterfront towers. What matters is the spread: there are roughly 582 rentals active on the Stamford-area MLS right now, and most renters are shopping the $1,800–$3,000 band — where the well-priced units lease within days. Here's the real breakdown, and where to find the value.

Short answer: Median asking rent in Stamford is around $3,850/mo, but that's inflated by the high end. In practice there are about 26 apartments under $1,800/mo, 82 under $2,200, and 215 under $3,000 across the roughly 582 active rentals. Budget-minded renters live in the $1,800–$3,000 band; the sub-$2,200 units go fast, so tour the day one lists and have your application ready.

What rent actually costs, by tier

Averages hide the market; the count by price band shows it. As of the latest MLS update, the Stamford-area rental inventory breaks down roughly like this:

  • Under $1,800/mo — about 26 units. The thin bottom shelf: studios and small one-bedrooms, mostly in Glenbrook, Springdale, and Belltown. These are gone in days.
  • Under $2,200/mo — about 82 units. The sweet spot for one-bedrooms outside the luxury core. Competitive but workable if you move quickly.
  • Under $3,000/mo — about 215 units. Opens up one- and two-bedrooms, including some newer buildings and downtown options.
  • $3,000 and up. Luxury and waterfront — Harbor Point, the South End towers, downtown high-rises with full amenities.

These counts move every few hours as units list and lease; the version on this page is baked fresh from the MLS on each refresh, and you can browse every one of them on the Stamford apartments for rent map.

Where the cheaper rent is

Rent in Stamford tracks distance from the water and the train. The more affordable rentals cluster in Glenbrook, Springdale, Belltown, and Newfield — quieter, a bit further from downtown, often in smaller buildings and two-families. The premium sits in Harbor Point, the South End waterfront, and the downtown high-rises, where you pay for amenities, walkability, and the shortest walk to the Metro-North station. If your budget is under $2,200, start your search in the first group; if amenities and a downtown walk matter more than price, the second.

Why Stamford rent beats NYC

A large share of the renters I work with are New Yorkers. The math is simple: a roughly 50-minute Metro-North ride to Grand Central buys materially more space per dollar than Manhattan or the inner NYC suburbs. That demand is exactly why the $2,000–$3,000 band is the most competitive part of Stamford's market — it's where "more space, same commute" lives. If you're moving up from the city, budget a little above the bottom tier and be ready to sign fast.

How to actually land a good one

In a market where well-priced units lease in days, speed is the whole game. Get your paperwork ready before you tour — proof of income, credit, references, and first/security in hand. Set up an alert so you see new listings the hour they hit the MLS rather than days later on a national portal. And know the building before you apply: ask about the actual all-in cost (some include heat/hot water, some don't), the parking situation, and the lease terms. I keep the live rental map updated every three hours so you're never chasing an apartment that's already gone.

Is rent in Stamford going up? Stamford rents have stayed firm on strong NYC-relocation demand and limited new supply at the lower end. The best hedge isn't timing the market — it's being ready to sign the day the right well-priced unit appears.
