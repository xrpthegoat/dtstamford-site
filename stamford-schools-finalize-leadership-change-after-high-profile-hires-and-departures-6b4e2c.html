Stamford Schools Finalize Leadership Change After High-Profile Hires and Departures

Stamford schools name Scott Clayton as new assistant superintendent for secondary education, ensuring stability amid recent leadership changes.

Uploaded · July 19, 2026 at 7:37 AM ET

The Stamford Board of Education has appointed Scott Clayton as assistant superintendent for secondary schools, effective immediately, following a unanimous vote on Tuesday night. This appointment comes after the district’s earlier selection of Matthew Laskowski for the same role was derailed when Laskowski accepted a principal position in Greenwich instead [4]. The change underscores the competitive nature of leadership roles in Connecticut’s public education sector and highlights the district’s need for continuity during a critical transition period ahead of the upcoming school year.

What's happening

The Board of Education confirmed Scott Clayton’s appointment after a unanimous vote on Tuesday night, making the role effective immediately [4]. Clayton, who previously served as principal of Scofield Magnet Middle School for 12 years, has been the district’s director of student data and systems since 2024 [4]. His new role will oversee middle schools, high schools, and adult and continuing education programs across Stamford Public Schools [4].

This appointment follows a period of instability: Matthew Laskowski was initially approved for the assistant superintendent position in June 2026, but withdrew from the role just days later to accept a principal position in Greenwich [4]. The board had previously approved two assistant superintendents for the upcoming school year—Lisa Saba-Price for elementary and Matthew Laskowski for secondary—on July 15, 2026, but Laskowski’s departure left the secondary role vacant until Clayton’s appointment [3].

Clayton brings a deep institutional history to the role, having served as a teacher, dean, and administrator within Stamford Public Schools for over two decades [4]. He holds a bachelor’s degree in history from Western Connecticut State University, a master’s in secondary education from the University of Bridgeport, and a superintendent certification from Central Connecticut State University [4]. His prior leadership at Scofield Magnet Middle School and his recent work in central office data systems position him to manage both instructional and operational challenges [4].

A leadership shift with implications for district stability

The swift replacement of Laskowski with Clayton reflects a broader pattern in Stamford’s public education leadership: high-demand roles are increasingly contested, and experienced local candidates are being prioritized to ensure continuity. While the district had initially planned for a two-tiered assistant superintendent structure for the upcoming academic year, the departure of one appointee has temporarily disrupted that plan, though the board’s swift action suggests strong internal coordination [3].

The fact that Clayton was already serving in a central office role—director of student data and systems—means he is not a new hire in the traditional sense. His familiarity with district-wide systems, student performance metrics, and administrative workflows likely made him a more immediate fit than an external candidate. This choice may signal a shift toward internal promotions for key leadership roles, especially in times of uncertainty.

The timing of the appointment—just weeks before the new school year—also underscores the importance of stability in school leadership. Sudden changes in central administration can affect morale among teachers and principals, particularly during budget planning and staffing cycles. By appointing a seasoned leader with deep roots in the district, the board appears to be prioritizing continuity over rapid change.

What this means for Stamford buyers & renters

For Stamford residents considering a move—whether to buy or rent—this leadership change is a subtle but meaningful signal about the city’s long-term stability and investment in public institutions. While the appointment itself doesn’t directly impact housing prices or rental availability, it reflects broader trends in municipal governance that influence neighborhood desirability.

Stamford’s public schools are a major factor in housing decisions, especially for families and professionals seeking walkable, transit-accessible communities. The fact that the district is actively managing leadership transitions with internal candidates suggests a level of institutional resilience. When school leadership changes are handled smoothly and with continuity, it reinforces confidence in the district’s ability to deliver consistent educational outcomes—something that matters deeply to homebuyers and renters alike.

Moreover, the appointment of a locally experienced leader like Clayton may indicate a preference for stability over disruptive innovation. This can be reassuring to residents who value predictability in public services, especially in a city that’s seen rapid development and rising costs. In neighborhoods with strong school reputations, this kind of leadership continuity can help maintain property values and rental demand.

The broader context of Stamford’s real estate market—characterized by high demand, limited inventory, and strong transit access—means that even small signals of institutional stability can influence buyer sentiment. With the city’s downtown core and waterfront areas continuing to attract professionals and young families, the perception of a well-managed school system can make a difference in whether a home is seen as a long-term investment or a short-term rental.

That said, the lack of public details on future budget allocations, staffing changes, or curriculum shifts means that buyers and renters should remain cautious about making assumptions. The board’s recent meetings have been marked by heated debates over budget cuts and staffing reductions, with several public comments left unaddressed [6]. While leadership appointments are positive, they don’t resolve underlying fiscal pressures that could affect school programs and, by extension, neighborhood quality.

In short, this appointment is not a headline-grabbing event, but it is a sign of institutional health. For those evaluating Stamford as a place to live, it adds a layer of confidence in the city’s ability to manage its most important public institutions—especially as the city continues to grow and evolve.
