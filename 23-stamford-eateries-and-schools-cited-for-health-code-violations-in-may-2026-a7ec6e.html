23 Stamford Eateries and Schools Cited for Health Code Violations in May 2026

23 Stamford eateries and schools cited for serious health code violations in May 2026, highlighting food safety concerns across the city.

Uploaded · July 14, 2026 at 4:27 PM ET

A recent wave of health inspections in Stamford has revealed serious sanitation and safety lapses across 23 food service operations, including five schools, raising concerns about public health and food safety standards. For residents who rely on local restaurants and school meal programs, this report underscores the importance of transparency and accountability in food handling—especially as summer dining and school year transitions approach.

What's happening

In May 2026, the Stamford Department of Health and Human Services conducted 60 food inspections, resulting in 23 entities receiving serious violations, a significant increase from the 10 priority violations issued in April [3]. These violations were categorized under a three-tiered system: priority violations (most serious, requiring correction within three days), priority foundation violations (less severe, corrected within 10 days), and core violations (least serious, corrected within 90 days) [3].

Among the cited entities were five schools, including Cloonan Middle School and Roxbury Elementary School [2]. The Villa at Stamford, a nursing home, was cited for one priority violation due to food stored above 41°F in a three-compartment fridge and one priority foundation violation for missing disposal date markings [3].

Restaurants also faced multiple citations. Lucy’s Pizza Parlor was cited for failing to maintain cold-holding temperatures below 38°F, along with several priority foundation violations related to refrigeration cleanliness, unlabeled chemical spray bottles, and lack of hygiene kits for vomiting or diarrhea incidents [3]. Sergio’s Pizza and Restaurant received five priority violations, including improper hand-washing before glove use and food contact surfaces that were not cleaned [3]. The Gartner Dining Center, operated by Flik Hospitality Group, was cited for uncovered food in a reach-in unit and broken equipment stored inside [3]. The facility stated in a follow-up email that all issues had been addressed per the inspection report [3].

The full list of establishments and specific violation details are available through the City of Stamford’s Food Inspection database [2], though the report does not include all names or addresses beyond those mentioned in news coverage.

Public health and food safety in context

The rise in serious violations—more than double the prior month—suggests a broader systemic challenge in maintaining consistent food safety standards across the city’s diverse food service landscape. While the city’s Environmental Health Inspections program is responsible for monitoring compliance [2], the fact that multiple schools and a nursing home were cited highlights the vulnerability of institutions serving vulnerable populations.

The lack of public response from several establishments, including Lucy’s Pizza Parlor and the Villa at Stamford, may reflect a gap in transparency, though the city’s reporting system does not require public comment from businesses post-inspection. The absence of detailed follow-up data—such as whether all violations were corrected in time—means the full impact of these findings remains unclear.

Still, the fact that priority violations must be resolved within three days indicates a clear regulatory expectation. The increase in citations may also reflect more rigorous or frequent inspections, though the city has not confirmed any change in inspection frequency or protocol.

That said, it’s critical not to overreact to isolated incidents. The fact that 23 entities were cited out of hundreds of food operations in Stamford doesn’t mean the city is failing. For buyers and renters, that’s a positive sign: it means accountability exists, and information is available.

As more residents become aware of inspection results, businesses may be more motivated to maintain high standards. Common questions

What types of violations were most common in Stamford's May 2026 inspections?

Common violations included improper food storage temperatures, lack of labeling, unclean refrigeration units, and failure to maintain hygiene protocols. Priority violations involved unsafe food holding temperatures and uncovered food in storage units.

How does this affect Stamford renters and buyers?

Renters and buyers should review the city’s food inspection database when evaluating properties, especially near schools or commercial areas. While isolated citations don’t define a neighborhood, repeated issues may signal broader operational concerns affecting livability and property value.

Where can residents access the full list of health inspection results in Stamford?

The City of Stamford’s Food Inspection database provides detailed reports on violations. Residents can access the full list at stamfordct.gov/food-inspection for transparency and due diligence.
